Life CFO — Verdict Types → Language Patterns (LOCKED)
Purpose of this map
To ensure that:
•	Answers feel confident but safe
•	Language is consistent across the product
•	Users learn to trust the tone implicitly
•	The AI never oscillates between styles or emotional registers
Each verdict type has:
•	A semantic meaning
•	A primary sentence pattern
•	Allowed variants
•	Explicitly disallowed language
________________________________________
1️⃣ CLEAR YES
Semantic meaning
The decision fits comfortably within current conditions without requiring trade-offs or vigilance.
Primary pattern (preferred)
“Yes — this fits comfortably within your current position.”
Allowed variants
•	“Yes — this is well within your current capacity.”
•	“Yes — this is financially comfortable right now.”
•	“Yes — this is fine to proceed with.”
Clarifier (optional)
•	“This does not rely on unusually tight timing or buffers.”
Disallowed
•	“Probably”
•	“Likely”
•	“As long as nothing changes”
•	Any numbers in the verdict sentence
________________________________________
2️⃣ YES, BUT NEEDS PLANNING
Semantic meaning
Achievable with intention, but not casually absorbable.
Primary pattern
“This is achievable, but it would need planning.”
Allowed variants
•	“Yes — but this would need planning to do safely.”
•	“This is possible, but not without preparation.”
•	“This can work, but only if it’s planned for.”
Clarifier (often useful)
•	“It would require setting aside funds ahead of time.”
Disallowed
•	“Risky” (too strong)
•	“Tight” (ambiguous)
•	“It depends” (non-decision)
________________________________________
3️⃣ NOT YET
Semantic meaning
The decision is directionally reasonable but premature under current conditions.
Primary pattern
“Not yet — this would be risky under your current setup.”
Allowed variants
•	“Not yet — this would stretch things too far right now.”
•	“Not yet — this needs a stronger buffer first.”
•	“This isn’t a good idea at the moment.”
Clarifier
•	“The timing is the issue, not the idea itself.”
Disallowed
•	“No, never”
•	“You shouldn’t”
•	Moral or judgmental language
________________________________________
4️⃣ NEEDS ATTENTION (NON-URGENT)
Semantic meaning
There is something materially relevant that requires awareness or action, but not alarm.
Primary pattern
“This needs attention before you commit.”
Allowed variants
•	“This is workable, but something needs addressing first.”
•	“There’s a gap here that needs resolving.”
•	“This deserves a closer look before deciding.”
Clarifier
•	“There’s a missing or uncertain piece that affects the outcome.”
Disallowed
•	“Urgent”
•	“Immediately”
•	“Dangerous”
________________________________________
5️⃣ NO (CLEAR)
Semantic meaning
The decision does not fit within current constraints even with planning.
Primary pattern
“No — this doesn’t fit within your current financial reality.”
Allowed variants
•	“No — this would create too much pressure.”
•	“No — this would compromise other priorities.”
•	“This isn’t viable right now.”
Clarifier
•	“It would require trade-offs that aren’t reasonable.”
Disallowed
•	“You can’t”
•	“You failed”
•	Absolute moral framing
________________________________________
6️⃣ INSUFFICIENT DATA
Semantic meaning
A confident decision cannot be made safely with current information.
Primary pattern
“I don’t have enough information to answer this safely yet.”
Allowed variants
•	“I’m missing a key piece to answer this properly.”
•	“This needs a bit more clarity before deciding.”
Clarifier
•	“Once that’s clear, I can give you a direct answer.”
Disallowed
•	Guessing
•	Defaulting to “No”
•	Asking multiple follow-up questions at once
________________________________________
7️⃣ REASSURANCE (CHECK-IN STYLE)
(Used for Home status memos, not direct questions)
Semantic meaning
No action required; system is stable.
Primary pattern
“Nothing needs attention right now.”
Allowed variants
•	“Things look steady.”
•	“You’re in a stable position.”
•	“No issues stand out at the moment.”
Disallowed
•	“All clear” (too binary unless clearly defined)
•	“Perfect”
•	“Safe forever”

🎛 Language Guardrails (Global)
Always
•	Calm
•	Adult
•	Decisive
•	Non-judgmental
Never
•	Legal disclaimers
•	Emotional reassurance loops
•	Over-verbosity
•	Numbers in the verdict sentence (unless unavoidable)
